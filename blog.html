Sega DoomCast ~ July 17, 2024

Ok Doomer

Yes, you are reading this correctly! Thanks to the homebrew SDK/Operating System for Dreamcast, Doom 64 is being ported and is now in the early stages of becoming playable on Sega's last home console, by JNMartin84 (of OpenLara N64 port fame in 2022).

Doom 64 originally launched for the N64 in 1997 and saw ports to modern consoles and PC back in 2020. As of July 16, 2024, you can now boot the beloved Nintendo 64 version on your homebrew enabled DC, experiencing the early stages of one of the highest critically acclaimed variants of Doom ever to grace a console.

There is work to be done, such as adding in enemies, audio and fixing the HUD and clipping, but the concept is sound and the stability of the port looks superb, and you can even try it out if you want to!

There are a few steps required to get Doom 64 working on the swirly console, but it's fairly straightforward once you have the KallistiOS (KOS) environment set up. Firstly, you need to obtain and use a Doom64.z64 ROM with the MD5: B67748B64A2CC7EFD2F3AD4504561E0E, put the ROM in the WADTOOL directory, and then run the script to build the *.ELF and generate the required *.WAD files. Finally, you need to build a self-bootable CD or load from a HDD, ODE, or SD card, using your preferred method.

Full instructions are linked below on JNMartin84's Github and shouldn't take more than 15 minutes from start to finish. The results are basic for now, but incredibly smooth nonetheless, take a look:

What is KallistiOS?

KallistiOS (spearheaded by Falco Girgis, aka Gyrovorbis and his team) is a pseudo-operating system for Dreamcast that allows for RAM upgrades, HDD, Lightgun, SD Card Reader support, Broadband Adaptor support, custom BIOS flashing, and in it's most recent updates,KOS now supports virtual filesystems amongst other things. Built on GCC (GNU Compiler Collection) toolchain, KallistiOS is capable of utilising libraries from C++ to Lua, SDL, and OpenGL/OpenAL to deliver a modern, programmer-friendly, developer environment.

Recently, Sonic Mania was ported to DC using KallistiOS, and demonstrated network streamed assets working with app-level code ported to the system by Michael Fadely, aka SonicFreak94, with an impressive 60 FPS locked performance!

Multiplayer Memories

I was on to something! ~ July 10, 2024
Multiplayer Memories

Innovation from Inspiration

When it comes to looking back over games you used to play, do you also recollect the ways you used to play them? Which modes did you favour, which controllers, and which configurations.

My friends and I hammered GoldenEye. To the point that we knew every spawn point, every weapon, and every quirk each other had, but the one thing we didn't have were separate screens. Everytime we played, there was the constant fear of screen-cheating, and there was no where to hide, no let up to the barrage of action, and games were more frantic in front of a TV sat on a sofa. We played two-player games more often than four-player because the screen size was larger, the frame rate was better, and the people waiting their turn got rowdy chanting and goading us on in the background.

Beyond the loadout, past the modes and the configurations, we strived for more competitive play, and over time I had various bright ideas that would revolutionise our sessions.

I would take a simple piece of paper, the width of my 26" Goodman's TV, and tape it across the middle of the TV screen to give each portion of the screen some privacy. The idea was simple, a divider, and with some seating adjustments, it worked, just about. Player one sat on a chair, and player two hunched down on the floor. The two of us could play on separate screens, but I wasn't happy with this creation it was too makeshift.

My Magnum Opus came as I remembered that my old 13" CRT was rotting up in the loft, and that there was a second 12" TV/VCR combo in my sister's room. There was a spark, and I immediately got to creating my ultimate gaming setup!

I placed two chairs back to back in the middle of my bedroom, and using one N64, I put a splitter on the RF cable, connected two more RF cables, I then hooked up one cable to one TV and another to the second. I placed the N64 between the two chairs, on the floor, this gave equal cable length to each player, and it kept the console close to the TV's to minimise any trip hazards. I plugged it in to test my work. It was like a miniature LAN party, but in 1998!

The final stage of this contraption was to place cushions in front of the TVs for each player to sit on, and then cover alternate halves of each TV with a piece of card. This meant that each player only had visual access to their half of the screen, and there would be no opportunity to cheat.

(Ikea style graphics courtesy of my wife)

Contraption Conclusion

GoldenEye, Perfect Dark, and Turok 2 multiplayer forced us to try out new tactics and rethink the usual way we played by having at least a little forewarning that someone was lurking around the corners. Sniping and closequater combat became edge-of-your-seat experiences because you simply couldn't nail down where someone was firing from, and you had no idea if anyone was creeping directly behind you.

I remember wanting to push this one further, with a four-way splitter, four chairs back to back, four TVs, bigger TVs (!), and four pieces of "L"-shaped cardboard to caudon off other players screens. I also remember the impracticality of borrowing four chairs, power strips, and four TVs, let alone making this for comfortable use in a bedroom.

Alas, a mythical four-way (play session) was not to materialise in my teenage bedroom.

Enter The Arena

Though my setup was technically smart, it required a few components most people wouldn't have had lying around, so I was fortunate to have had the bits needed and patient friends who would help me test out my contraption.

The Computer-Systems Arena was an almost comically priced $63 piece of hardware that deployed mirrors to reflect portions of the screen at their respective players. It's not wholly elegant, but it's effective once jostled into place, and it could work on up to 27" screens.

Reviews of this peripheral were relatively positive at the time (2000), with the only unavoidable complaint being the amount of space required to set it up properly. The positivity from this new challenging way to play oozes from the majority of the other reviews, which stated that it felt weird not knowing where people are and makes the game feel akin to PC multiplayer, i.e. one screen per player.

The Arena was a commercial product that would have given a similar experience to the contraption I built a few years prior. This suggests to me that I wasn't alone in wanting this type of experience from my gaming sessions, though sadly, I never so much as saw an Arena in real life, let alone heard of its existence, until very recently.

ReCollect64 Reviews: #8

InterAct Arcade Shark ~ July 5, 2024
Arcade Shark

A Sharks Tale

Recently I performed a short analysis on the N64's catalogue and noted that of the 388 titles, there are 36 fighting games. That accounts for nearly 10% of the Nintendo 64's entire library, and so it makes sense that none other than InterAct would jump at the opportunity to make a peripheral for the 64-bit wonder.

The InterAct Arcade Shark does exactly what it says on the tin: it's an arcade stick in all of its form and function and doesn't attempt to be anything else!

Conceived in 1996, the N64 wouldn't exactly have had too may games around the time of launch. War Gods, Ki Gold, MK Trilogy, and perhaps even the lacklustre MK Mythologies Sub-Zero would have been all that was available in the first year, so it was slim pickings until 1997, when MK4 and Mace the Dark Ages arrived. 1998 was even better for fighting games, with the likes of Fighters Destiny, Bio F.R.E.A.Ks, G.A.S.P., and Flying Dragon appeared. It wasn't until midway through the N64's lifespan, 1999, that the first installment of the wildly popular fighting series Super Smash Bros. was released.

I managed to bag mine a few months ago after lusting after one for many years. For the pincely sum of £34.99, I won a boxed one in immaculate condition, and with PriceCharting stating that the current loose price averages £31 and a boxed price circles roughly £89, I was pretty pleased with my purchase!

Being that this was produced in 1996, I can verify that this sadly doesn't work with a Rumble Pak. I tried plugging it in to a compatible game and it just sent the analog stick doolally.

Fresher than a Thresher

With titles such as the four Mortal Kombat games, Killer Instinct Gold, Super Smash Bros. and Fighters Destiny at the fore of the N64's fighting roster, there are some real bangers on the system.

I tested out the aforementioned titles, and I have to say the Arcade Shark is a pretty fierce piece of kit, but I have a few qualms. The buttons feel great, but they're not raised enough on the C-Buttons, it would have been great to have all of the face buttons given the same treatment, just coloured appropriately. Instead we have the A and B buttons along with the L, R, and Z buttons standing proud like real arcade buttons, whereas the C-Button cluster is markedly smaller and flatter. The net result is that your fingers gravitate towards the L and R buttons, which, in KI Gold and most fighting games on N64, do nothing.

Other games fare similarly with the same button-feel issues rearing their heads. Perhaps it's a matter of muscle memory, and perhaps I need to throw a lot more time into fighting games (I rarely play them), but as a casual device, I found those C-Buttons a bit annoying.

The stick is great, it's responsive, and it works across both the D-Pad and analog stick slots, but, and this is a big but, the stick tended to twist loose during play. I had to really screw it in hard to prevent any movement, because in the heat of a fighting game you really do push those little ball-top sticks to their upper limits, and you tend to unknowingly tilt harder when under the pump.

Don't get me wrong, the stick feels great, but it needed a little refining to be perfect. The fact that only one stick is supplied is also a possible issue. A spare is always welcome, but would you ever need to plug two in at once? The sensible answer is no, but the inquisitive answer is; it would have been cool to have the option.

No game really sees you switching from stick to D-Pad and back again, so it's a moot point, however, were you to play a game like Mario Kart 64 and want to steer with one and flick the other to adjust your camera or the HUD, it would have been nice. As it stands, the circular base you screw the stick into can be used as a flat D-Pad or analog stick without the stick plugged in, which is handy.

I suppose this is a better method than having just one stick slot and a switch to swap the D-Pad function to analog stick or vice versa, but the downside of that idea is that you couldn't use the screw in stick in the middle position to make the entire pad work more comfortably for smaller hands in all games.

The Arcade Shark is oversized, and it weighs well over a kilo, so it's best to use it on a surface rather than your lap for best effect, unless you enjoy dead legs.

The Pros:

 Solid arcade style fighting stick
 Also great for Shoot 'em ups

The Cons:

 Only one stick supplied
 The C-Buttons are too flat
 Not Rumble Pak compatible

Conclusion:

InterAct's Arcade Shark hits hard as a fight stick, but has a few annoying traits. If you're a fan of the fighting genre then this is a great purchase to really get the coin-op experience from the utra hardware, but if you're thinking about using it on any other genre of game, think again.

ReCollect64 Reviews: #7

PicoCart64 Lite V1.4 ~ July 3, 2024
PicoCart64 Lite V1.4

A Mighty Micro Machine

When Konrad Beckmann came up with the PicoCart64 concept, it was pretty novel. Building a working flashcart for N64 that costs next to nothing and was easily buildable at home. It was something of a tinkerer's dream.

Now that it has breached concept stage, there are three main versions of the PicoCart64 available to construct, so I opted for the 1.4 lite because it's the most stable version available right now. I may have to upgrade to a v2 down the line to check in with any updated features when they happen!

  • PicoCart64 v1 lite - The lite version using a Raspberry Pi Pico board capable of booting homebrew. This is the most user-friendly option as of now.
  • PicoCart64 v1 - The stamp version using a RP2040 Stamp from Solder Party. This is used as an experimental development platform for the next-gen version.
  • PicoCart64 v2 - The next-gen version which has two RP2040 chips, on-board PSRAM chip supporting ROMs up to 64MB, a WiFi module and SD-card support. This is still heavy in development. Don't build this unless you want to develop it.

The mainboard can be purchased from the Lectronz Store for $5 and the Pi Pico for just $4 direct from RaspberryPi.com. That's the GBP equivalent of £8 before shipping and tax if bought direct from the stores and assembled yourself. It works out a little more (roughly £10-15 + shipping) if you buy one preassembled from a third party vendor.

By simply slotting the RPi into the mainboard and applying a little solder to each of the 40 legs, you'll build yourself a very affordable device that fits in your N64 and is fully flashable!

Clever but Ultimately Arduous

The PicoCart64 is smart but goes no further than granting a very streamlined experience, and there are no bells or whistles to be seen here. Preparing the hardware is very easy, and the process for flashing a game to the unit is identical. However, if you plan on playing multiple games in quick succession, it gets very jarring very quickly, as there is no luxury of a game select screen, cheats, or save management.

The upshot for this is that you can on one game, drill into it, and complete it without distraction or the temptation to instantaneously switch to something else. Because you can't.

Instructions for flashing/ROM loading:

  1. Download the latest firmware (Latest or Stable).
  2. Unzip either picocart64-v1-ntsc.uf2 or picocart64-v1-pal.uf2 depending on your N64 region.
  3. Hold down BOOTSEL and connect the Pico to your computer via USB-C.
  4. Copy over the *.UF2 file to the Pico.
  5. Once the file is copied over the Pico will reboot. It is now ready for homebrew.
  6. On the UF2 ROM Builder page, click "Choose file", and select the N64 ROM you want to use (must be in z64 format).
  7. Keep "Compress" checked unless you have a reason for using an uncompressed ROM.
  8. Press "Build UF2" and wait until the process is done.
  9. Hold down BOOTSEL and connect the Pico to your computer via USB-C.
  10. Copy over the *.UF2 file to the Pico.
  11. Once the file is copied over the Pico will reboot.
  12. Disconnect the PicoCart64 from your computer and insert it into the N64 console with the Pico facing towards the BACK of the console.
  13. Power on the N64 console as normal and your game will load.

ROMs must be in z64 format (big endian), and in order to play some games, you may need to use Paladin or MotetIII to adjust the CIC. I managed to get it working on both my NTSC-J (CIC 610X) and PAL (710X) consoles with relative ease, and with ROM hacks working as well as retail games.

Barring having some issues with ROMs converting on the site, the PicoCart64 surprised me in just how straight forward the procedure is get to playing your legally backed up games. The big downside is that all the converted games need to be kept and stored elsewhere, like on your PC, as they cannot be stored onboard the device.

The proposed v2 version with twin RP2040s and SD card support could probably be programmed with a rudimentary interface to select raw, unconverted, ROMs to load into memory. Sure, it would cost a little more, but it would definitely be handy if it ever makes it out of development.

I could even conceive a version of the v1.x firmware that utilises onboard WIFI to network load ROMs, but sadly the developer himself, Konrad Beckmann, has sunset the PicoCart64 project, so no more updates will come for the forseeable future, unless someone else picks up the torch and runs with it.

The Pros:

 Incredibly cheap option
 Easy to assemble and flash

The Cons:

 ROM building can be slow
 Developement has ceased

Conclusion:

While the PicoCart64 definitely strikes at the hearts of the budget conscious, it lacks mainline features and offers a very bare-bones experience. It's fun and friendly to use, but the novelty wears off quite quickly with repeated conversion and re-flashing. You most definitely get what you pay for in the world of N64 flashcarts.

Deep Diving

Whilst building ReCollect 64's main collection app, I have devised list after list of things I need to add, fix, or look into. I recently found some time to focus on one subject, so I began looking into Operation Cards, the foldable piece of paper included with the manual, and any other items of paperwork you may find in a boxed game. Not every game came with one, and not all regions had them. I didn't remember having any whilst collecting a PAL set (though I focused on carts over CIB), and I don't own a single one in any of the games I've purchased boxed from any other regions.

Operation cards, or quick start guides, are designed to give you the bare basics it would take to perform the majority of moves, or abilities of your character depending on the game. Wave Race 64, for example, lays out the instructions for how to change the camera angle, shift your weight on the Jet Ski, or dampen the waves with the "B" button. The flip-side of the page explains all the tricks and stunts you can do, it's a compact and colourful way to quickly reference important features without having to read tonnes, or hold a manual in front of you while you play.

When I began searching, my first Google hit came from The Chozo Project and the writers footing into USA Operation card hunting. I noted several things when scouring that site, firstly, it's a great insight into the owners collecting mentality, but secondly, it felt a tad untapped. Sure, it was a tremendous starting point, but I knew there were more out there to discover.

With this thought lodged firmly in my brain, I contacted a person I knew would have some insight into the matter, an absolute fountain of knowledge: @JosetteTrinkets. We had a lengthy conversation regarding these over the course of several days, over which time we really dug into these cards and got a grasp on what games had them, and in which regions. We exchanged countless messages exclaiming a new find, and eventually figured out ways to mine Nintendo's own Virtual Console servers for scanned treasure.

Finding them was as simple as combining an NUS ID into the known URL. For example, https://m1.nintendo.net/docvc/NUS/JPN/NCZJ/NCZJ_J.pdf opens up the Custom Robo V2 Operation Guide and manual, so editing the end of the URL to NUS/JPN/NWRJ/NWRJ_J instantly bagged us the JPN Operation card for Wave Race 64 (as an example), though changing it to NUS/USA/NWRE/NWRE_E (USA) or NUS/EUR/NWRP/NWRP_P (European) only holds manuals for these regions 🙁.

Super Mario 64 GBA Port

Portable Plumber Power! ~ June 29, 2024
SM64 GBA Port

Super Mario 64 Advancing

Less than a month ago, Joshua Barretto set the GBA and N64 scenes ablaze with news of something we all thought would be impossible: Super Mario 64 was being ported to the Game Boy Advance.

Since early May, the visuals have gone from a primative red triangle to a rudimentary silver model of the plumber to a bendable, animatable facsimile of the main man himself. The progress is wonderful to behold, and the coder behind it is doing some very impressive work in delivering an instantly recognisable version of the original for the 2001 handheld.

The gravity of this statement is unimaginable, probably even for Nintendo, who no doubt will want to stomp all over this and DMCA the heck out of it. Hopefully this will fly under their radar and continue undetected.

The word "Port" implies that Joshua is taking the decompiled code and recompiling it for GBA, but this doesn't appear to be the case. He is not taking the 64-bit binaries and simply converting them to 32-bit. Rather, there is a lot of custom code, no pre-built engines in use, and what appears to be a lot of trial and error in getting the game to render. Understandably, performing this feat is going to be extremely challenging, and there has to be a heck of a lot of optimisations and specific techniques to get a version anywhere close to the original, so it may be more accurate to consider this a de-make, rather than a port.

Improvement in Movement

The latest iteration of this project sees Mario regain some of his distinguishing acrobatics, with flipping, crouching, sliding, and jumping looking extremely recognisable. What's impressive is how the developer is gradually adding features that gave the game its distinctive look and feel, and he's attempting to rebuild every element within the restrictions of the 240 × 160 aperture.

The Game Boy Advance was limited to just 288 KB RAM and 98 KB VRAM, whereas the N64 has access to 4 MB (or 8 MB for games that were created to take advantage of the Expansion Pak when inserted). The SOC design of the GBA means that onboard was an ARM7TDMI running 16.78 MHz and a Sharp SM83 running 4 / 8 MHz, whereas the N64 housed a dedicated 64-bit NEC VR4300 CPU running at 93.75 MHz, paired with a dedicated SGI RCP GPU running 62.5 MHz. A huge difference in technology, and a vast difference in architecture, all of which would make directly porting a 3D 64-bit home console game to a traditionally 2D 32-bit handheld extremely challenging.

The fully bespoke approach taken, to effectively recreate the entire game from scratch with only graphic resources to hand and an understanding of how SM64 looks, feels, and plays, will be no doubt tougher, but is already yielding visible results at a lot faster rate than a traditional port would see.

Hopefully this game will flourish and allow yet another console to enjoy the 1996 platformer, and that's what matters at the core of this: that everyone can enjoy their favourite games any way they like.

I will be continuing to follow this project as it evolves and will be sure to keep you up to date on any significant updates going forward!

N64s Catalogue

Quality over Quantity ~ June 27, 2024
N64 Game Info

'The N64 Had No Games'

I have heard this a lot throughout the years. People would sound off that the SNES had 1,700+ releases and that there were a startling 4000+ games on Sony's first PlayStation console, but the N64 had "f#&k all." I would typically retort with "What percentage of those SNES/PSX games have you actually played?" and the answer was always very few, perhaps a handful of games, but definitely no where near a significant percentage of the available titles.

Sony's PlayStation and Nintendo's Super Nintendo had a lot going for them, particularly RPG games, movie tie-ins, and light guns! During the 90's, they were fierce rivals, with the whole reason the first PlayStation existed being for Sony to spite Nintendo after the sourness of their illfated SNES-CD partnership.

Rivalry is a good thing in the industry because it nurtures progression, innovation, and most importantly, variety. But back then, obtaining 1000+ games was impossible for most people, and within each console's respective catalogue was undoubtedly a percentage of dud titles. The bigger the catalogue, the higher number of games that small percentage represents.

With the N64, Nintendo utilised their "seal of quality" to best effect, ensuring endless torrents of sub-quality games didn't show up on their platform and thus the number of games quickly dimished. That, plus Ninetndo 64's cartridge-based approached to distribution as opposed to CD, meant smaller or more optimised games and higher development and manufacture costs.

If you wanted your game on a Nintendo, it had to be good! There were even games like Resident Evil 2 that is today deemed the impossible port, because of the incredible amount of optimisation and skill it took to whittle down 2 CD's worth of data (757 MB to be precise) into a tiny 64 MB cart.

Disecting the N64 Catalogue

As a collector, I like to know how many games it takes to complete my collections, so I recently broke this down and discovered that of the N64's 388 individual titles, within that you have (roughly) 83 Sports titles, 65 Racing, 49 Action, 36 Fighting, 25 Platform, 20 Simulation, 18 Shoot 'em up, 16 Adventure, 16 First-Person Shooter, 14 Puzzle, 14 Board Games, 9 Strategy, 8 Party, 3 Card-based, 1 Rhythm, and a couple more miscellaneous ones that slip through an indescript net. I have classed these fairly loosely because there are arguments for and against calling the Zelda games RPGs or action-adventure games, and while both are true, which is really the most accurate categorisation? Recompilation aims to immediately recompile binaries without the need for lengthy (upwards of years) decompilation beforehand, followed by months of porting to other platforms via their respective SDKs. The Freshest Flashcart ~ Jun 04, 2024
SummerCart64

Whats New With You?

As part of my ongoing N64 collection, I have recently purchased the SummerCart64, created by Mateusz Faderewski (aka Korgeaux), a brand new flashcart that not only plays your N64 ROMs and homebrew, but also plays 64DD ROMs without the need for conversion!

Krikkz EverDrive 64 X7 has been my go-to for a while now, since I upgraded just after it was released in 2019, and before that I happily used the EverDrive 64 2.5 without issue. SummerCart64 stands on the shoulders of giants and takes multiple strides forward, building on everything that's great about the EverDrive series of carts and adds in features such as native 64DD emulation and heaps of developer forward features.

It should be noted that the SummerCart64 I bought was from Phemon Mod, meaning the casing is the version sold by Mena. Each store and supplier has their own build quality as it is assembled by a third-party, but the SummerCart64 hardware was designed by Korgeaux, and the N64FlashcartMenu software was developed by Korgeaux and Networkfusion (Robin Jones), as well as multiple other contributors, including Meeq (Christopher Bonhage) and Suprapote!.

Some people will argue that clones are better value, and easier to get hold of. I have never bothered with knock-off devices because though they get the job done, they always seem to have some form of hiccup when it comes to durabilty and software support. I know plenty of people that have purchased a cheaper clone only to have it fail or quite literally fall to pieces just months down the line. This £120 pre-built model is extremely convenient, though for much less the unit is entirely self-buildable, 3D printable, and flashable!

The build quality of this Mena built SummerCart64 feels really nice; it uses a sleek plastic finish on its smoky grey exterior that feels wonderful even though it doesn't have the tactile texturing of the OEM carts. The cut-outs for the Micro SD card and the USB-C connector are recessed nicely (note: the SD card slot is miniscule and difficult to remove once inserted), and the hex bolt screws are a nice solid touch to hold it all together.

The first thing I noticed about the SummerCart64 shell is that there is a somewhat gangly button on the rear that clunks around a bit, it really needed to be spring-loaded, or just less rattly, to push it up even higher in the build-quality stakes buy the assembler, in this case Mena. This button can be configured to change 64DD disks, sending USB packets, or raising N64 interrupts for devs.

SummerCart64 Features:

  • High N64 ROM compatibility (Morita Shogi 64 & Mario no Photopi hardware not supported yet)
  • 64DD hardware emulation: loads 64DD disks (+ ROM combo/multi Disk)
  • UltraCIC_C: region switching (enhanced)
  • Up to 2TB Micro SD card supported
  • Comprehensive ROM save database (including HomeBrew headers)
  • Comprehensive ROM information display
  • Real Time Clock support (CR2032 battery)
  • Emulator support (NES, SNES, GB, GBC) ROMs
  • Saves: EEPROM 4/16 kbit, SRAM 256/3x256 kbit, FlashRAM/SRAM 1 Mbit
  • Auto writeback after ~1s to the Micro SD/USB
  • N64 ROM box image support
  • Music playback (MP3)
  • Background image (PNG) support

Sleek Hardware with Great Software

After formatting your Micro SD card to EXFAT or FAT32, you will need to download the latest sc64menu.n64 and copy it to the root to load the OS on your virgin SD card. Using a PC and card reader to drag and drop whatever you want onto the device and make any folders you wish is very simple, though you can also use the sc64deployer app on PC. The only stipulation is that the 64DD IPL files need to be stored in a folder called "menu".

Selecting N64 and 64DD ROMs is straight forward, select with A and then load with A, however, playing games like the F-Zero X Expansion Kit requires you to do the following: Select the N64 cartridge ROM (F-Zero X [J].n64), back out with B (don't load the game with A), select the F-Zero Exp Kit dump (NUD-EFZJ-JPN.ndd), then hit R to load the pair in tandem. While this works fine and only REALLY applies to F-Zero X and Dezaemon 3D, I still feel that there should be a simpler way to do this, it feels a little clunky in its execution.

At first, for the life of me, I could not get the thing to connect to my PC, so I couldn't set the RTC or update the firmware. I later found out through @LuigiBlood that there were specific Windows drivers required to communicate to the device, and this allowed me to flash the update and sync the RTC to my PC with ease.

The sc64deployer app is for loading ROMs, updating, backing up, and debug terminal access. It's a command-line app and relies on commands to operate, but luckily, the SummerCart64 Documentation covers everything you could possibly need to know. All I needed were the commands: "sc64deployer.exe firmware update sc64-firmware-v2.18.1.bin" and "sc64deployer.exe set rtc" to get me where I wanted to be with the device.

The load speed of ROMs is extremely fast from the SD card, and from my testing: EVERY ROM I threw at it worked perfectly without having to convert a single thing. Games like Morita Shogi 64 and Mario no Photopi are just about the only ones don't work on N64FlashcartMenu yet because they require further individual hardware emulation, but that's the same with any other flashcart out there, including the EverDrive X7. Backing up saves for sharing or modifying also worked brilliantly well and was exceptionally fast!

As you can see, the SummerCart64 is packed full of features and also includes ROM information with boxart support as well as compatibility for emulators within the N64FlashcartMenu OS. The emulators I used include Neon64 V2 for NES, Sodium64 for SNES, GB64 for Game Boy & Game Boy Color, and TotalSMS for Sega Master System/Game Gear and SG1000 (not currently working)! These emulators worked to a point; the SNES emulator is a little less refined, and there were some layer issues in games like Super Mario All-Stars + World but they played well enough to be enjoyable!

The SummerCart64 is an incredible device, with blisteringly fast load times, compatibility across all regions for a vast selection of ROMs, plus the ability to play games like F-Zero 64 with the Expansion Kit without the need for the 64DD itself! What's more, the SummerCart64 project is completely open source meaning you will see community improvements, forks, and more, making it truly future-proof.

Mateusz Faderewski is the creator of SummerCart64's core hardware, firmware, and supporting software, and they have done a truly incredible job here. Bearing in mind that the SC64 began its life as a device for creating homebrew, its an incredibly well rounded package that can do virtually everything!

You cant re-map the buttons either so it feels a little off, but on the other hand games like AeroFighters and even Knife Edge Nosegunner utilise the Z-Trigger for your main guns and it feels superb!

Handling in-game feels nice, really nice, and the pitch and yaw of this stick really builds the immersion when playing games that best suit this equipment. In actual fact ANY game could theoretically be played with this controller, including Mario Kart 64, Goldeneye, or even Zelda if you REALLY wanted to, but I wouldn't. Barring a couple of these fringe-case flight sims the NitroStick doesn't feel great for RPG/FPS/Platforming play, as the learning curve becomes huge, so stick to the perfectly formed trident-controller for those games!

The Pros:

 Feels great for flight games.
 Exceptional precision.

The Cons:

 No remappable buttons.
 Limited use for games.

Conclusion:

It's not for everyone, nor is it for every game, but I kind of like Quickshot's quirky stab at a realistic flight stick.

The iQue Player

A plug-and-play N64 ~ May 29, 2024
iQue Player

Interesting Retro Tech

Not only will this site offer information on your favourite console, I also aim to fill your mind-hole with info on a plethora of accessories and interesting related devices too.

In Y2K China's government imposed a ban on games consoles, so Nintendo had to think outside the box to crack the market. The iQue player is an oddity in that it is a console that is built into a controller, and it only plays N64 games that have been paid for and downloaded to the memory card. This system was to combat rampant piracy in China, and it worked up until as recently as 2019, when it was hacked by Normmatt, Marshallh, Stuckpixel, Riley/ROL and SUXXORS.

The iQue had just 14 games available in its life time, these were:

  • Animal Crossing
  • Custom Robo
  • Dr. Mario 64
  • Excitebike 64
  • F-Zero X
  • Mario Kart 64
  • Paper Mario
  • Sin and Punishment: Successor of the Earth
  • Star Fox 64
  • Super Mario 64
  • Super Smash Bros.
  • The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time
  • Wave Race 64
  • Yoshi's Island

The Legend of Zelda: Majoras Mask was meant to be release too, which even featured as a screenshot on the iQues box, but it never happened. Additionally a Traditional Chinese Translated version of Ocarina of Time was found ready for release in Taiwan (ROC) but it never happened.

A Beautiful Design

When I bought mine in 2021 I was elated to have finally got my hands on one of my unicorns (I have a list I'll explain in another post). The console was notably different feeling to an N64 controller, due to the missing middle prong and the more traditional form factor. It was smoother, sleeker heavier, and kind of nice! I booted it up thinking that not a lot would happen, and luckily I had the paired memory card supplied with it. I was buzzing to find not only Wave Race 64, and Dr. Mario, but also Mario Kart and OOT!.

The games are all localised in Chinese and so look incredibly different when they boot up, and I find that fascinating to observe!

The iQue could be the ultimate way to play N64 games, for its simplicity and form factor. Nintendo sound definitely revisit this system-on-a-chip model if they ever make an N64 "classic" because it is ideal for pick up and play and the analog stick feels really nice too! The only caveat is lack of multiplayer without an additional device, but these days Nintendo could implement online instead, HDMI out, and possibly let you use your Bluetooth N64 controllers for the Nintendo switch on it for local multiplayer.

The 64-bit possibilities are endless!
