ReCollect64 Reviews: #15

Tsurikon Fishing Controller ~ Sep 22, 2024
Ascii Tsurikon 64

Fishing for Compliments

When the N64 initially came out, the only fishing I was interested in was the Ocarina of Time minigame. I used to really enjoy trying to get the 19-LB lunker and, of course, obtaining that elusive heart piece!

It never occurred to me as a teenager that dedicated fishing games existed, they weren't even a blip on my radar until a mate got the Dreamcast with Sega Bass Fishing and a fishing rod controller! We played that game a lot in 1999, but it still wasn't a high priority for me to fish on my N64, probably because I didn't have a fishing controller for it, and most of the fishing games would have been costly Japanese imports.

The N64 only had a small number of dedicated fishing games, including Nushi Tsuri 64, Nushi Tsuri 64 Shiokaze Ninotte, In-Fisherman Bass Hunter 64, Bass Masters 2000, and Itoi Shigesato no Bass Tsuri No. 1, the latter of which received its own dedicated controller!

The Ascii Tsurikon 64 (or Tsuricon 64) is an N64 controller in the shape of a fishing rod, but it also encapsulates a real-feel reel with adjustable drag, rumble via 2xAA batteries, and a motion sensor that is triggered when casting and setting the hook!

It wasn't the only aquatic peripheral around, but it was definitely the most realistic looking and the most difficult to get your hands on, as it was a Japanese exclusive.

Reely Al-luring

This controller was primarily designed for Itoi Shigesato no Bass Tsuri No. 1 but can function on the other fishing titles too with basic controls. In Itoi Shigesato no Bass Tsuri No. 1, the reel even has a realistic clicky nature to it, and the whole device simulates the feel of fishing with a rod beautifully.

The rod pad feels great in hand; it's balanced and intuitive to use. The C-buttons are in a rubbery concave configuration, which is odd at first but suits the game perfectly. The other buttons are also well placed; a lot of care and attention has gone into the ergonomics of this controller, and it pays dividends while in-game.

I'm also glad of the decision to anchor the controller's cable at the bottom of the controller, rather than the top, because the temptation to *thwhip* the cable around your living room would be phenominal and dangerous. So dangerous.

Sadly, the Tsurikon 64 doesn't have all the normal buttons an OG controller has. It is missing the D-Pad and the L button, which means some other non-fishing titles cannot be played on this quirky controller. Barring those, you're free to play games like Mario Kart 64 and Wave Race 64 on it regardless!

As a peripheral, its so niche! It's plainly not designed for use on any old game, but it's perfect, and I mean flawless in its design of simulating a fishing rod. When it came out in 1999, it retailed for ¥5800, which is just £30. Nowadays the Tsurikon costs upwards of £60-£80 depending on condition, and even then it is usually unboxed.

The Pros:

 Satisfyingly clicky reel
 Built in rumble
 Sensor for casting

The Cons:

 Not all fishing games support the reel or sensor!

Conclusion:

I only managed to obtain one of these recently, and it came unboxed with a free copy of Itoi Shigesato no Bass Tsuri No. 1. but now I wish I had had one years ago when they were fresh and new!

Ascii's Tsurikon is a novel input device that does exactly what it says on the tin. Fishing enthusiasts will unquestionably fall in love with this device hook, line, and sinker!
